## 1. Introduction and Overview

Financial planning is the process of setting personal financial goals, understanding your current position, and building a strategy to move from where you are to where you want to be. In the UK, the financial landscape is shaped by a specific tax system, state-provided benefits (including the State Pension and NHS), and a range of regulated savings products such as ISAs and workplace pensions.

A well-constructed financial plan covers five interlocking areas:

1. **Cash flow** — ensuring that income exceeds outgoings and that surplus is directed purposefully.
2. **Protection** — ensuring that unexpected events (illness, death, job loss) do not derail long-term plans.
3. **Debt management** — understanding the cost of all liabilities and prioritising repayment.
4. **Wealth accumulation** — growing assets tax-efficiently over time.
5. **Decumulation** — drawing on accumulated wealth in a tax-efficient and sustainable way in retirement.

A financial plan is a living document. It should be reviewed at least annually, and after any major life event: a new job, a pay rise, marriage, children, property purchase, inheritance, or approaching retirement.

## 2. Income and Expenses

### 2.1 Gross Salary and Deductions

Most UK employees receive a gross salary from which deductions are made before take-home pay is received. The primary deductions are:

- **Income Tax** — charged on earnings above the Personal Allowance (£12,570 in 2025/26).
- **National Insurance (NI)** — employee Class 1 contributions, charged on earnings between £12,570 and £50,270 at 8%, and above £50,270 at 2%.
- **Pension contributions** — typically deducted at source under a salary sacrifice or relief-at-source arrangement.
- **Student loan repayments** — deducted via PAYE once income exceeds the plan-specific threshold.

For example, a gross salary of £51,000 in 2025/26 produces approximately:

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Gross salary | £51,000 |
| Income tax | £7,486 |
| National Insurance | £3,096 |
| Net take-home (before pension) | £40,418 |

### 2.2 Budgeting and the 50/30/20 Framework

A practical budgeting framework divides take-home pay into three categories:

- **50% — Needs**: rent or mortgage, utilities, groceries, transport, minimum debt repayments.
- **30% — Wants**: restaurants, holidays, hobbies, subscriptions, non-essential clothing.
- **20% — Savings and investments**: pension, ISA, emergency fund, additional debt repayment.

These proportions are indicative and will vary by life stage. Early in a career, someone with student debt and a desire to save a deposit may push savings closer to 30–40%. Someone approaching retirement may be in a position to save 40–50% of income.

### 2.3 Tracking Expenses

Accurate expense tracking is the foundation of a reliable financial plan. Categorise all outgoings into:

- **Essential fixed costs** — mortgage/rent, insurance premiums, council tax, minimum debt payments.
- **Essential variable costs** — food, utilities, fuel.
- **Discretionary costs** — dining out, holidays, entertainment.
- **Financial goals** — pension contributions, ISA contributions, savings.

Many people underestimate spending by 20–30% when estimating from memory. Reviewing three months of bank statements gives a realistic baseline.

### 2.4 Salary Growth

The UK average salary growth has historically been approximately 2–4% per year in nominal terms, broadly matching or slightly exceeding inflation. For financial planning purposes, a conservative nominal salary growth assumption of 3% per year is reasonable over long time horizons.

## 3. Assets and Liabilities

### 3.1 Asset Classes

Personal financial assets fall into several broad categories:

| Category | Description | Typical Growth | Liquidity |
|----------|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Cash | Bank accounts, savings accounts | 1.5–4% | Immediate |
| Stocks & Shares ISA | UK/global equities and bonds held in an ISA | 5–8% p.a. (long run) | 2–5 days |
| Workplace / SIPP pension | Invested pension funds | 5–8% p.a. (long run) | From age 57 |
| Residential property | Primary home or buy-to-let | 3–5% p.a. | Months |
| General Investment Account | Equities, bonds, funds outside tax wrappers | 5–8% p.a. | 2–5 days |
| Business interests | Private company shares, sole trader assets | Highly variable | Illiquid |

Growth rates above are long-run real return estimates and will vary significantly year to year. Past performance is not a guarantee of future results.

### 3.2 Net Worth

Net worth is the fundamental measure of personal financial position:

> **Net worth = Total assets − Total liabilities**

A young professional with a mortgage will often have a negative or low net worth for years, even while building good financial habits. The trajectory matters more than the absolute level at any given point.

### 3.3 Debt Management

Not all debt is equal. Debt can be categorised by its cost and strategic importance:

| Debt Type | Typical Interest Rate | Priority |
|-----------|----------------------|----------|
| Credit card (revolving) | 20–30% APR | Highest — pay off immediately |
| Personal loan | 5–15% APR | High |
| Car finance | 4–10% APR | Medium |
| Mortgage | 2–6% APR | Low — long-term management |
| Student loan (Plan 2) | RPI + 3% (capped) | Lowest — treat as a graduate tax |

**The debt avalanche method** — paying minimums on all debts and directing surplus cash to the highest-interest debt first — minimises total interest paid. **The debt snowball method** — paying the smallest balance first regardless of rate — provides psychological momentum but costs more in interest.

For mortgages, overpayments of 10% per year are typically allowed without early repayment charges. Even modest overpayments significantly reduce total interest paid and shorten the term.

### 3.4 Student Loans

UK student loans operate differently from commercial debt. Repayment is income-contingent and does not appear on standard credit checks. Key rules for **Plan 2** (started university 2012–2023):

- Repayment threshold: £27,295 per year
- Rate: 9% on income above the threshold
- Write-off: 30 years after first due repayment date
- Interest: RPI + up to 3% (sliding scale based on income)

For most graduates who will not repay the full balance, the loan functions like an additional marginal income tax rate of 9% rather than a traditional debt. Voluntary overpayments are almost never financially optimal for Plan 2 holders who will not fully repay.

## 4. UK Taxation

### 4.1 Income Tax (2025/26)

Income tax is charged on earnings, pension income, savings interest, and dividends above the Personal Allowance.

| Band | Income Range | Rate |
|------|-------------|------|
| Personal Allowance | Up to £12,570 | 0% |
| Basic Rate | £12,571 – £50,270 | 20% |
| Higher Rate | £50,271 – £125,140 | 40% |
| Additional Rate | Over £125,140 | 45% |

**Personal Allowance taper**: The Personal Allowance reduces by £1 for every £2 of income over £100,000. By £125,140 the allowance is fully withdrawn, creating an effective marginal rate of 60% on income between £100,000 and £125,140. Pension contributions that reduce adjusted net income below £100,000 can recover the Personal Allowance.

**Dividend income** is taxed at reduced rates: 8.75% (basic rate), 33.75% (higher rate), and 39.35% (additional rate) above the £500 dividend allowance.

**Savings interest** is covered by the Personal Savings Allowance: £1,000 for basic-rate taxpayers, £500 for higher-rate, and £0 for additional-rate.

### 4.2 National Insurance

Employee Class 1 National Insurance in 2025/26:

| Range | Rate |
|-------|------|
| Up to £12,570 | 0% |
| £12,571 – £50,270 | 8% |
| Over £50,270 | 2% |

Employer NI is also payable at 13.8% on earnings above the Secondary Threshold (£5,000 from April 2025), though this does not reduce take-home pay directly. Salary sacrifice pension arrangements reduce both employee and employer NI, making them more efficient than net-pay arrangements for many employees.

### 4.3 Capital Gains Tax

CGT is charged on gains realised from the disposal of assets such as shares, investment funds, and property (other than the primary residence under Private Residence Relief).

| Asset Type | Basic Rate Taxpayer | Higher/Additional Rate Taxpayer |
|------------|--------------------|---------------------------------|
| Investments (shares, funds) | 10% | 20% |
| Residential property | 18% | 24% |

The **Annual Exempt Amount** (AEA) for 2025/26 is £3,000. Gains below this threshold are not taxed.

Key planning strategies:
- **Use both spouses' allowances** — transfers between spouses/civil partners are exempt.
- **Bed & ISA** — sell investments and immediately re-purchase inside an ISA to shelter future growth.
- **Timing** — spread disposals across tax years to use the AEA each year.
- **Capital losses** — losses can be offset against gains in the same or future tax years (they must be reported to HMRC within four years).

### 4.4 Inheritance Tax

IHT is charged at 40% on the value of an estate above the available nil-rate bands. The key thresholds for 2025/26:

| Band | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Nil-Rate Band (NRB) | £325,000 |
| Residence Nil-Rate Band (RNRB) | £175,000 |
| Combined (single person with property) | £500,000 |
| Combined (married couple, both unused bands) | £1,000,000 |

The RNRB applies when a residence is left to direct descendants (children, grandchildren). It phases out at a rate of £1 for every £2 by which the estate exceeds £2 million.

**Charitable giving**: Leaving at least 10% of the net estate to charity reduces the IHT rate from 40% to 36%.

**Gifts and the 7-year rule**: Outright gifts to individuals are Potentially Exempt Transfers (PETs). If the donor survives 7 years, the gift falls entirely outside the estate. Taper relief reduces the tax on gifts made 3–7 years before death.

| Years since gift | Fraction of IHT payable |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| 0–3 years | 100% |
| 3–4 years | 80% |
| 4–5 years | 60% |
| 5–6 years | 40% |
| 6–7 years | 20% |
| 7+ years | 0% |

**Exempt transfers**: Gifts to a UK-domiciled spouse or civil partner are wholly exempt. The annual gift exemption allows £3,000 per year (with carry-forward of one unused year). Small gifts of up to £250 per person per year are also exempt.

**Pensions and IHT**: Pension pots are generally outside the estate for IHT purposes until 2027 (and potentially under a revised framework thereafter — take specialist advice). Nominating beneficiaries on pension arrangements is important.

### 4.5 Stamp Duty Land Tax

SDLT (in England and Northern Ireland) is payable on property purchases:

| Property Value | First-Time Buyer Rate | Standard Rate |
|---------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Up to £250,000 | 0% | 0% |
| £250,001 – £925,000 | 5% | 5% |
| £925,001 – £1,500,000 | 10% | 10% |
| Over £1,500,000 | 12% | 12% |

First-time buyers pay no SDLT up to £425,000 and 5% on the portion from £425,001 to £625,000. For properties over £625,000 the standard rates apply in full.

Additional property (buy-to-let or second home) attracts a 3% surcharge on the full purchase price.

## 5. Retirement Planning

### 5.1 State Pension

The full new State Pension in 2025/26 is £11,973 per year (£230.25 per week). This is subject to the triple lock, which means it increases each year by the highest of: CPI inflation, average earnings growth, or 2.5%.

To receive the full State Pension you need 35 qualifying National Insurance years. A partial pension is paid with 10–34 qualifying years. Gaps can be filled by voluntary Class 3 NI contributions (currently £824.20 per year), which can be an excellent investment for those with gaps from career breaks, self-employment, or time abroad.

The State Pension Age is currently 66 for both men and women. It will increase to 67 between 2026 and 2028, and is expected to rise further to 68 in the 2040s.

### 5.2 Workplace Pensions and Auto-Enrolment

Auto-enrolment requires all employers to enrol eligible workers into a workplace pension. The minimum total contributions from 2019 onwards are:

- **Employee**: 5% of qualifying earnings (including tax relief)
- **Employer**: 3% of qualifying earnings

Qualifying earnings in 2025/26 are based on income between £6,240 and £50,270. Many employers offer enhanced matching — for example, matching contributions up to 5% or 10% of salary. **Always contribute enough to claim the full employer match** — it is effectively a 100% instant return.

### 5.3 Self-Invested Personal Pensions (SIPP)

A SIPP is a personal pension that gives the holder full control over investment choices. Contributions receive basic-rate tax relief at source (the provider claims 20% from HMRC). Higher and additional-rate taxpayers can claim further relief via Self Assessment.

| Tax Status | Pension Contribution | Effective Net Cost |
|------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Basic rate (20%) | £100 | £80 |
| Higher rate (40%) | £100 | £60 |
| Additional rate (45%) | £100 | £55 |

The Annual Allowance for pension contributions is £60,000 in 2025/26 (or 100% of earnings if lower). Unused allowances from the three preceding tax years can be carried forward. Contributions above the allowance are subject to an annual allowance charge (effectively removing the tax relief).

The **Money Purchase Annual Allowance (MPAA)** of £10,000 applies once you have flexibly accessed your pension, restricting future pension contributions significantly. Take advice before flexible drawdown if you plan to continue contributing.

### 5.4 Defined Benefit vs Defined Contribution

**Defined Benefit (DB)** pensions promise a specific income in retirement, usually calculated as:

> Annual pension = (Years of service × Accrual rate) × Final or average salary

For example, a scheme with a 1/60th accrual rate and 30 years of service would provide 30/60 = 50% of the relevant salary. DB pensions typically include inflation indexation and spouse's benefits. They are increasingly rare outside the public sector.

The transfer value of a DB pension is expressed as a CETV (Cash Equivalent Transfer Value). Transferring a DB pension to a DC arrangement requires regulated financial advice for CETV values above £30,000.

**Defined Contribution (DC)** pensions accumulate a pot of money invested in markets. The retirement income depends entirely on investment performance, contribution levels, and longevity. Most modern workplace pensions are DC arrangements.

### 5.5 Pension Access and Drawdown

From age 57 (rising from 55 in 2028), pension funds can be accessed flexibly:

- **Tax-free cash**: Up to 25% of the fund (capped at £268,275 lifetime) can be taken tax-free. This can be taken as a lump sum or in stages alongside drawdown.
- **Flexi-access drawdown**: Remaining funds stay invested and income is drawn as needed, subject to income tax.
- **Annuity**: The pension pot is exchanged for a guaranteed income for life. Annuity rates depend on age, health, interest rates, and chosen features (inflation linking, spouse's pension).

A common drawdown strategy is to draw just enough each year to use the full Personal Allowance (£12,570) tax-free, supplemented by ISA withdrawals (which are always tax-free) for additional spending needs.

### 5.6 Required Pot Size

A common rule of thumb is the **4% rule**: withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one of retirement and increase withdrawals with inflation each year. Under this rule, a £1 million pot can sustain approximately £40,000 per year indefinitely (historically, in balanced portfolios).

For a more precise calculation, consider:

> **Required pot** = (Desired annual income − State Pension − DB income) × 25

For example, a desired income of £35,000 with a full State Pension of £11,973:
> (£35,000 − £11,973) × 25 = £575,675

This is a rough starting point. The actual amount depends on expected returns, inflation, longevity, and sequencing risk. A financial planning tool with Monte Carlo simulation gives a more robust estimate.

## 6. Investment Growth

### 6.1 Compound Growth

The power of compound growth is the single most important concept in long-term wealth building. The formula for the future value of a lump sum is:

> **FV = PV × (1 + r)^n**

Where PV is the present value, r is the annual growth rate, and n is the number of years.

For a £10,000 investment at 7% per year:
- After 10 years: £19,672
- After 20 years: £38,697
- After 30 years: £76,123

The rule of 72 is a useful shortcut: **divide 72 by the annual growth rate to estimate the number of years to double**. At 7%, money doubles approximately every 10.3 years (72 ÷ 7 = 10.3).

### 6.2 Inflation and Real Returns

Nominal returns must be adjusted for inflation to assess real purchasing power. If a portfolio grows at 7% nominally while inflation is 2.5%, the real return is approximately 4.4% (strictly: (1.07 / 1.025) − 1 = 4.39%).

For long-term planning, use real returns when expressing goals in today's money, or use nominal returns when projecting in future nominal amounts. Mixing the two leads to errors — be consistent throughout a plan.

### 6.3 Asset Allocation

Asset allocation — the split between different asset classes — is the primary driver of long-term portfolio performance. Risk and expected return generally go together:

| Asset Class | Expected Real Return | Volatility |
|-------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Global equities | 4–6% p.a. | High |
| UK equities | 3–5% p.a. | High |
| Bonds (government) | 0–2% p.a. | Low–Medium |
| Property (REITs) | 3–5% p.a. | Medium |
| Cash | 0–1% p.a. | Very low |

A classic rule of thumb for equity allocation is **100 minus age** (so a 40-year-old holds 60% equities). Modern guidance tends to favour higher equity allocations for longer investment horizons, since younger investors have time to recover from market downturns.

### 6.4 Diversification

Diversification reduces risk without necessarily reducing returns. Within equities, hold funds that invest in thousands of companies across multiple geographies and sectors. Index funds and ETFs provide low-cost diversification:

- **Global equity index fund**: Tracks the MSCI World or FTSE All-World index, providing exposure to 1,500–4,000 companies across developed markets.
- **Emerging markets fund**: Adds exposure to faster-growing developing economies.
- **Bond fund**: Reduces portfolio volatility and provides income.

The correlation between asset classes matters: assets that do not move together provide better diversification. Government bonds and equities have historically had low or negative correlation during equity bear markets, though this relationship has occasionally broken down during inflationary shocks.

### 6.5 Investment Costs

Investment costs compound just like returns — but in the wrong direction. A 1% higher annual charge over 30 years can reduce a portfolio by 25–30%.

Costs to monitor:
- **Ongoing Charges Figure (OCF)** of funds: aim for below 0.20% for passive index funds.
- **Platform charges**: typically 0.15–0.45% of assets, capped at a flat fee for large portfolios.
- **Adviser charges**: typically 0.5–1.0% of assets per year for ongoing advice.
- **Transaction costs**: minimised by buy-and-hold strategies with low turnover.

## 7. Goal-Based Planning

### 7.1 Framework

Goal-based planning organises financial objectives into:

1. **Identify the goal** — be specific: "Buy a three-bedroom house in Bristol for £350,000 in 2027."
2. **Quantify the cost** — including all associated costs (stamp duty, legal fees, surveys).
3. **Set a target date**.
4. **Choose a funding strategy** — savings, mortgage, loan, inheritance, or a combination.
5. **Calculate the required monthly saving**.
6. **Review progress** annually.

Goals compete for the same finite income. Prioritisation is necessary. A simple framework ranks goals by:
- **Time horizon** — short-term goals (under 5 years) require capital preservation; long-term goals can tolerate more risk.
- **Flexibility** — a house purchase has a flexible timeline; school fees have a fixed deadline.
- **Importance** — distinguish "must have" from "nice to have."

### 7.2 House Purchase

Buying a property typically requires:
- **Deposit**: minimum 5%, typically 10–25% for favourable mortgage rates.
- **Stamp Duty**: see Section 4.5.
- **Legal fees**: typically £1,500–£3,000.
- **Survey**: £400–£1,500 depending on type.
- **Moving costs**: £1,000–£3,000.

For a £350,000 property with a 10% deposit:
| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Deposit | £35,000 |
| Stamp Duty (first-time buyer) | £5,000 |
| Legal fees | £2,000 |
| Survey | £800 |
| Moving | £1,500 |
| **Total** | **£44,300** |

Mortgage affordability is typically assessed at 4–4.5× annual income (though lenders vary). A £350,000 mortgage requires an income of approximately £78,000–£87,500. Joint applications allow combined incomes to be used.

### 7.3 Education Costs

University costs in England (2025/26):
- **Tuition fees**: up to £9,535 per year (three-year course: ~£28,605).
- **Maintenance loan**: up to ~£13,000 per year in London.
- **Total debt at graduation**: typically £45,000–£60,000 for Plan 5 students.

Parents saving for children's education often use Junior ISAs (JISA, limit £9,000 per year per child), which are tax-free throughout. A JISA invested from birth at 7% per annum would grow to approximately £314,000 by age 18 at maximum contributions.

### 7.4 Major Purchases and One-Off Goals

For major purchases (cars, home renovation, holidays), the choice between saving in advance and borrowing depends on the interest rate and time horizon:

- If the savings interest rate exceeds the loan rate (rare), save in advance.
- If borrowing is cheaper than the opportunity cost of deploying savings elsewhere, borrow selectively.
- Cars depreciate rapidly — prefer to pay cash or take low/zero-interest finance rather than high-rate personal loans.

## 8. Risk Management

### 8.1 The Purpose of Insurance

Insurance transfers the financial consequences of low-probability, high-impact events to an insurer in exchange for a regular premium. The principle: **insure risks you cannot afford to self-insure**.

- You can absorb a £200 phone repair — do not insure it.
- You cannot absorb 20 years of lost income from disability — insure it.

The most important personal insurances in the UK:

### 8.2 Life Insurance

Life insurance pays a lump sum or income to beneficiaries upon the policyholder's death. It is primarily relevant for people with financial dependants (a partner, children, or anyone who relies on the insured's income).

**How much cover?** A common starting point:
- Enough to repay all outstanding debts (mortgage, loans).
- Plus 5–10 years of income replacement for surviving dependants.

For a 35-year-old with a £250,000 mortgage, a partner, and two young children, a sum assured of £600,000–£800,000 is not unreasonable.

**Types**:
- **Level term**: Fixed payout throughout the policy term. Simple and transparent.
- **Decreasing term**: Payout reduces over time, designed to track a repayment mortgage.
- **Whole of life**: No expiry — ensures a payout, often used for IHT planning.

**Writing in trust**: A life policy written in trust pays directly to beneficiaries without forming part of the estate, avoiding IHT and probate delays.

### 8.3 Income Protection Insurance

Income Protection (IP) replaces a proportion of income (typically 50–70%) if the policyholder cannot work due to illness or injury. It is the single most under-owned important insurance in the UK.

Key features:
- **Deferred period**: The waiting period before payments start (4 weeks, 13 weeks, 26 weeks, or 52 weeks). Longer deferred periods reduce premiums.
- **Payment period**: Until recovery, a fixed term, or retirement age.
- **Definition of incapacity**: "Own occupation" (unable to do your specific job) is the most generous definition.

IP premiums are influenced by age, occupation, health, deferred period, and payment term. A 35-year-old in a professional occupation might pay £30–£80 per month for cover that pays £2,000–£3,000 per month.

### 8.4 Critical Illness Cover

Critical Illness (CI) pays a tax-free lump sum on diagnosis of a specified serious illness (typically cancer, heart attack, or stroke). It complements Income Protection — IP covers ongoing income loss; CI provides a capital sum for major adjustments (modifying a home, paying off a mortgage, private treatment).

### 8.5 Emergency Fund

An emergency fund is a readily accessible cash reserve for unexpected costs or income disruption. It is the foundation of financial resilience — without one, any unexpected event forces expensive borrowing or premature divestment of long-term investments.

**Recommended levels**:
- **Employed**: 3–6 months of essential expenses.
- **Self-employed / variable income**: 6–12 months of essential expenses.
- **Business owner with employees**: 6–12 months (covering business and personal).

The emergency fund should be held in an easy-access savings account, not in investments. During periods of market volatility, the last thing you want is to be forced to sell investments at a loss to fund an emergency.

**Building the fund**: If starting from zero, treat the emergency fund as the first savings priority, ahead of long-term investment (except always contribute enough to pension to get the employer match). Automate a monthly transfer on payday.

### 8.6 Buildings and Contents Insurance

Buildings insurance is required by all mortgage lenders and covers the cost of rebuilding the property structure. Contents insurance covers household possessions. The sum insured should reflect rebuild cost (not market value) for buildings and the replacement value of possessions for contents.

## 9. Sample Profiles

### 9.1 Profile A — Graduate at Career Start

**Alice, 26, London, Junior Analyst**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Annual salary | £32,000 |
| Monthly take-home | ~£2,270 |
| Assets | ISA: £8,000; Pension: £4,500; Cash: £2,000 |
| Debts | Student loan (Plan 2): £52,000 |
| Net worth | ~£14,500 (excl. student loan as unlikely to repay fully) |

**Key priorities**:
1. Build emergency fund to 3 months of expenses (target: ~£6,000).
2. Contribute to workplace pension to capture full employer match.
3. Begin regular ISA contributions (even £200/month compounds significantly over 40 years).
4. Avoid high-interest consumer debt.
5. Consider income protection insurance as dependants are unlikely now, but income is already essential.

**Projection (30 years to age 56)**:
Assuming 3% salary growth and consistent 7% ISA/pension growth, Alice could have:
- Pension pot: ~£420,000
- ISA: ~£350,000
- Total investable assets: ~£770,000

### 9.2 Profile B — Mid-Career Family

**Ben and Sarah, both 40, Yorkshire**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Combined salary | £95,000 (Ben: £55,000; Sarah: £40,000) |
| Assets | Primary home: £350,000 (mortgage: £210,000); ISA: £75,000; Pension (combined): £180,000; Cash: £25,000 |
| Debts | Mortgage: £210,000; Car finance: £8,000 |
| Net worth | ~£412,000 |

**Key priorities**:
1. Maximise pension contributions — both are in higher-rate tax band or close to it, so pensions are extremely tax-efficient.
2. Maintain 6 months emergency fund (~£15,000 for this household).
3. Review life and income protection cover — two children and a mortgage mean the financial consequences of death or disability are severe.
4. Plan for children's university costs using Junior ISAs.
5. Consider using surplus income to overpay mortgage vs invest — at current interest rates, the mortgage rate may exceed bond returns, making overpayment attractive.

**IHT position**: Estate worth ~£622,000. Above the £500,000 combined NRB + RNRB for a single person, but if each spouse holds half, assets pass to the surviving spouse free of IHT, and the full £1,000,000 combined allowance applies on second death.

### 9.3 Profile C — Pre-Retirement

**Carol, 58, South East**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Annual salary | £68,000 |
| Assets | Primary home: £650,000 (mortgage-free); Pension (SIPP + workplace DC): £520,000; ISA: £180,000; Cash: £35,000 |
| Debts | None |
| Net worth | ~£1,385,000 |

**Key priorities**:
1. Maximise pension contributions for the final 7–9 working years — the tax relief at 40% is extremely valuable.
2. Plan decumulation strategy: draw pension to the top of the basic-rate band (£50,270) combined with ISA withdrawals for additional spending.
3. Review IHT position — estate is approximately £1,385,000. With a spousal transfer (if applicable) the combined threshold of £1,000,000 may leave ~£385,000 exposed to 40% IHT. Consider making gifts, writing life policies in trust, and reviewing will.
4. Plan for long-term care costs — care costs in England can exceed £70,000 per year. A dedicated fund or care annuity should be considered.
5. Pension lifetime allowance — the LTA was abolished in 2024 but monitor future legislative risk.

**Retirement income at 67**:
- State Pension: £11,973
- Pension drawdown (4% of £800k projected fund): ~£32,000
- Total before ISA: ~£44,000
- ISA top-up: as needed (tax-free)

## 10. Updating and Maintaining Your Plan

### 10.1 Annual Review

At minimum, review your plan once a year. A good trigger is shortly after the tax year end (April), when you have a full picture of the prior year's income, tax, and savings.

Checklist for an annual review:
- [ ] Update income and expense figures.
- [ ] Update asset values (check pension and ISA statements, property estimates).
- [ ] Review debt balances and check for refinancing opportunities.
- [ ] Compare actual savings rates against targets.
- [ ] Check pension contributions and whether you are on track to meet retirement goals.
- [ ] Review insurance cover — is it still adequate given changes in income, dependants, or debts?
- [ ] Check beneficiary nominations on pensions and life policies.
- [ ] Review goals — have timelines or costs changed?

### 10.2 Life Events Requiring Immediate Review

Certain events should trigger an immediate plan review:
- **Change of employment** — new salary, new pension, new benefits.
- **Marriage or civil partnership** — joint planning, updated wills, beneficiary nominations.
- **Birth or adoption of a child** — new dependant; review life and IP insurance; start Junior ISA.
- **Separation or divorce** — financial settlement; pension sharing orders; new solo plan.
- **Inheritance** — lump sum; consider IHT implications of estate planning.
- **Property purchase or sale** — major shift in asset/liability profile.
- **Serious illness** — access to protection insurance; review drawdown plans.
- **Approaching retirement** — shift from accumulation to decumulation; annuity vs drawdown decision; State Pension deferral.

### 10.3 Keeping Records

Maintain a secure record of:
- Pension policy numbers and provider contact details.
- Life and income protection policy documents.
- Will and power of attorney.
- Property deeds and mortgage documents.
- ISA and investment account statements.
- National Insurance record (check at gov.uk/check-national-insurance-record).

Ensure a trusted person knows where these records are held.

## 11. Caveats and Limitations

### 11.1 This Application is Not Financial Advice

This application and guide are provided for educational and illustrative purposes only. The projections, calculations, and analysis are simplifications of a complex reality and should not be relied upon as the sole basis for financial decisions. They do not constitute regulated financial advice under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA).

For personalised advice on pensions, investments, insurance, and tax, consult a qualified Independent Financial Adviser (IFA). Check that any adviser is authorised and regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) via the FCA Register at https://register.fca.org.uk.

### 11.2 Simplifications Made

The calculations in this application make several simplifying assumptions:

- **Tax rates**: The 2025/26 rates and bands are used. These change every tax year.
- **Inflation**: A fixed 2.5% inflation rate is assumed. Actual inflation varies significantly.
- **Growth rates**: Assumed growth rates are long-run averages. Actual returns will vary, and sequence-of-returns risk is significant near and in retirement.
- **Pension rules**: The Lifetime Allowance (LTA) was abolished in 2024 but the application does not model all interactions with historic protections.
- **Student loan**: The model does not account for interest accrual on the loan balance.
- **State Pension**: The model uses a fixed State Pension amount; actual entitlement depends on NI record.
- **Income sources**: Only employed salary is modelled. Self-employment, rental income, dividends, and other income sources are not separately modelled.

### 11.3 Legislative Risk

UK tax law changes frequently. The rates, bands, allowances, and rules described in this guide are correct for the 2025/26 tax year as understood at the time of writing. Future changes — particularly to pension tax relief, IHT, and ISA allowances — may significantly affect the projections. Always obtain up-to-date professional advice for significant financial decisions.

### 11.4 Modelling Limitations

Long-term financial projections carry substantial uncertainty. A 30-year projection with a fixed growth rate is a simplified model of a deeply uncertain future. Use projections as a directional guide, not a precise forecast. Run multiple scenarios (pessimistic, base, and optimistic) and revisit the plan regularly.
